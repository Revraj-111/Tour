Qila Mubarak

The Blessed Fort | Itihaas Da Gawah

Standing tall for over 1800 years, Qila Mubarak is not merely a fort — it is the soul of Bathinda. Its weathered kachhi-pucca walls have witnessed the rise and fall of empires, the courage of warriors, and the anguish of royalty. This is where history breathes.

Did You Know? Qila Mubarak is believed to be one of the oldest surviving forts in India, with some historians dating its origins to the Kushana period (circa 90-110 AD). The fort's unique construction using unbaked bricks has allowed it to withstand the test of time.

Itihaas | The Historical Journey

90-110 AD

Kushana Era Origins

The fort is believed to have been founded during the Kushana dynasty, making it one of India's most ancient fortifications. Raja Dab is credited with its early construction.

1000-1200 AD

Rajput Stronghold

The fort served as a strategic military outpost for Rajput rulers, controlling the crucial trade routes between North and Central India.

1240 AD

Razia Sultan's Imprisonment

Perhaps the fort's most poignant chapter — Delhi's first woman Sultan, Razia Sultan, was imprisoned here after being defeated. Her story of courage echoes through these walls even today.

1754 AD

Sikh Kingdom Era

The fort came under the control of Sikh Misls, particularly Maharaja Ala Singh of Patiala, marking a new chapter in Punjab's history.

Present Day

Living Heritage

Today, Qila Mubarak stands as a protected monument, welcoming visitors from across the world who come to touch the stones that witnessed centuries of Indian history.

Architecture | Nirman Kala

The fort's architecture is a marvel of ancient engineering. Built primarily with kachhi-pucca (unbaked bricks), the construction technique has ensured its survival through centuries of harsh weather and warfare. The massive walls, some rising to 30 feet, create an imposing silhouette against Bathinda's skyline.

Key architectural features include the ten imposing gateways, intricate bastions positioned at strategic points, underground passages rumored to connect to other parts of the city, and the central courtyard where royal durbars were once held.

Razia Sultan | Ek Bahadur Kahaani

The name Razia Sultan is forever etched into the stones of Qila Mubarak. In 1240 AD, after being defeated in battle and separated from her beloved Altunia, Sultan Razia was imprisoned within these very walls. Despite her tragic fate, her courage, intelligence, and determination remain an inspiration.

She was not just India's first female Sultan — she was a visionary ruler who defied the norms of her time. Within these walls, she faced her darkest hours, yet her legacy shines brighter than ever.

"The fort speaks in whispers of her courage. Walk through its corridors, and you can almost hear the echoes of a queen who refused to be forgotten."
